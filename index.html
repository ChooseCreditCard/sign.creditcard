What does it mean to “sign” a card transaction?

Historically, signing a receipt was the core of cardholder authentication. Today, “signing” has expanded into a much broader concept: proving that you are the rightful cardholder using one or more factors.

  • Signature – handwritten verification still used in some regions, mostly declining.
  • PIN – numeric code known only to the cardholder; dominant in chip-and-PIN regions.
  • Biometrics – fingerprint/FaceID used for mobile wallets and PSD2-compliant flows.
  • Device authentication – secure elements & tokenized card credentials.
  • SCA logic – Strong Customer Authentication rules that require 2 factors.

In practice, “signing” now means confirming a transaction with whatever method your device, issuer or region requires.

How authentication works for card payments

Regardless of the method, most card authentication flows share the same building blocks:

  • 1. Identify the card – via chip, magstripe, NFC token, or online credentials.
  • 2. Verify the cardholder – signature, PIN, biometrics or 3-D Secure challenge.
  • 3. Apply rules – exemptions, risk scoring, SCA exceptions, issuer logic.
  • 4. Approve or decline – issuer decides based on risk and authentication level.

Issuers now combine risk models with SCA logic, meaning low-risk transactions often skip unnecessary authentication, while higher-risk ones require stronger proof.

The main authentication & signature models

Different card programs and regions use different default authentication types:

Signature-based transactions

Mostly legacy; still exists in some regions and on some terminals. Low security; being phased out.

Chip & PIN

The dominant method in Europe & many global markets. Strong offline and online verification.

Tap & go (low-value)

Contactless payments under a threshold often skip PIN; issuer risk rules apply in the background.

Biometric wallet authentication

Apple Pay / Google Pay authenticate you with biometrics, then send tokenized card data.

3-D Secure / SCA

Online authentication via app push, biometrics, or SMS-based OTP depending on region & issuer.

Risk-based “frictionless” approvals

No challenge required when issuer models determine the transaction is low-risk.

Risks, limits & security factors in authentication

Authentication affects approval rates, fraud resistance and user friction. Here are the main trade-offs:

Area Impact What to check
Fraud risk Weak authentication (e.g. signatures) increases fraud exposure. Prefer chip-and-PIN or wallet biometrics for higher-risk transactions.
Approval rates Biometrics + SCA can improve approvals by meeting regulation requirements. Ensure your issuer supports modern authentication methods.
Friction Some methods slow the checkout process. Wallets usually offer the fastest, strongest authentication.
Limits Contactless low-value limits vary by region and issuer risk rules. Know your tap thresholds; expect occasional “PIN required”.
Device trust Tokenized wallet transactions depend on device security. Keep OS updated; enable biometrics and device lock.
Offline acceptance PIN and chip support offline fallback; biometrics do not. Carry a physical card when traveling internationally.
